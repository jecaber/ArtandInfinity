The Great Pyramid of Giza

The Golden Ratio appears in one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World—the Great Pyramid of Giza (2580–2560 BC). In this structure, the ratio of the slant height to half the length of the base is approximately 1.618, closely matching the Golden Ratio. While it remains debated whether this was intentional or a remarkable coincidence, the mathematical harmony of the pyramid continues to intrigue historians and architects. Centuries later, the Ancient Greeks also showed a fascination with this ratio. Notable figures such as Phidias, Plato, and Euclid explored and applied it in various forms, from sculpture to geometry.

Sonata No. 1 in C Major

Composers have been using the Fibonacci Sequence and the Golden Ratio for hundreds of years to compose amazing music. Mozart’s sonatas, specifically the Sonata No. 1 in C Major, are often cited as examples of the golden ratio in music.
A sonata typically has three parts: exposition, development, and recapitulation. In the first movement of Mozart’s Piano Sonata No. 1 in C Major, the exposition has 38 bars, and the development and recapitulation have 62 bars. 62 divided by 38 equals 1.63 which closely matches the Golden Ratio. This creates a naturally pleasing sense of balance and progression, suggesting Mozart may have intuitively composed with these proportions in mind.

Fully booked hotel? Look no more.

Hilbert's Hotel, a thought experiment by David Hilbert, demonstrates that seemingly impossible feats are possible with infinite sets.
It illustrates that a fully occupied hotel with an infinite number of rooms can still accommodate new guests, even an infinite number, by cleverly rearranging the existing occupants.
